Camera Calibration

A camera is a machine that collapses 3D reality into a 2D image. Calibration is how we understand — and undo — that collapse.

Pinhole model Intrinsics & Extrinsics Lens distortion Zhang's method OpenCV implementation
Core idea: Camera calibration determines the mathematical relationship between 3D world coordinates and the 2D pixel coordinates in your image. Once calibrated, you can measure the real world, reconstruct 3D structure, and remove lens distortion.
§01 — Motivation

Why calibrate a camera?

When a camera photographs a scene, it performs a projection: a lossy, geometry-dependent transformation from 3D space to a 2D grid of pixels. This transformation depends on physical properties of the lens and sensor — and unless we know those properties precisely, we can't do much meaningful geometry.

📐

3D Reconstruction

Structure from Motion, stereo depth, SLAM — all require knowing the intrinsic parameters to triangulate real 3D positions from 2D pixel matches.

📏

Real-world Measurement

Measuring the size or distance of objects from images requires knowing how many pixels correspond to 1 mm in the scene — which comes directly from focal length.

🔭

Undistortion

Wide-angle and fisheye lenses warp straight lines into curves. Calibration gives us distortion coefficients to computationally straighten them.

🤖

Augmented Reality

Overlaying virtual objects onto a video feed requires knowing exactly how the camera sees the world — pose estimation depends entirely on a calibrated camera.

Key output of calibration: Two things — the intrinsic matrix K (what the camera's optics do) and the distortion coefficients dist (how the lens warps the image). Optionally also the extrinsic pose [R|t] for each calibration image.
§02 — The Camera Model

The Pinhole Camera Model

The foundational model for most cameras is the pinhole camera. Imagine a box with a tiny hole (the pinhole) on one side and an image plane on the opposite side. Light rays from the world pass through the pinhole and strike the image plane, forming an inverted projection of the scene.

Even though real cameras use lenses (not literal pinholes), the pinhole model accurately captures the geometry of projection. Lenses simply add distortion that we handle separately.

The Projection Formula

A 3D point P = (X, Y, Z) in camera coordinates projects to a 2D image point as:

Pinhole projection (camera coords → image coords)
u = fx · X / Z + cx
v = fy · Y / Z + cy
where (u,v) are pixel coordinates, (fx,fy) are focal lengths in pixels, and (cx,cy) is the principal point
Division by Z: This is the depth division that makes distant objects appear smaller. A point at Z=10m projects half the distance from center as the same point at Z=5m. This is perspective projection.

World Coordinates

The reference frame of the scene. This can be anything: the corner of a room, the center of a calibration target, or a GPS coordinate. Points in the world are (Xw, Yw, Zw).

Camera Coordinates

A coordinate frame centered at the optical center of the camera (the pinhole). The Z-axis points out along the optical axis. This transformation is handled by the extrinsic parameters.

Normalized Image Plane

A virtual plane at Z=1 in camera coordinates. After dividing by Z, we get dimensionless coordinates (x, y) = (Xc/Zc, Yc/Zc). This is where lens distortion is applied.

Pixel Coordinates

The final result: integer (or float) coordinates on the image sensor grid. The intrinsic matrix K maps normalized image coordinates to pixels via scaling (focal length) and translation (principal point).

§04 — Intrinsic Parameters

The Camera Matrix K

The intrinsic matrix K (also called the camera matrix or calibration matrix) encodes all the optical and geometric properties of a camera that are internal to it — independent of where the camera is placed in the world.

§05 — Extrinsic Parameters

Extrinsic Parameters [R | t]

While intrinsic parameters are about the camera's optics, extrinsic parameters describe where the camera is in the world — its position and orientation. Together they form the 3×4 matrix [R|t].

Extrinsic matrix [R | t] — 3×4
[R | t] =
[
r11r12r13tx r21r22r23ty r31r32r33tz
]

Rotation Matrix R

R is a 3×3 orthogonal matrix (RTR = I, det(R) = 1). It encodes the orientation of the camera frame relative to the world frame — essentially 3 rotation angles. R has only 3 degrees of freedom (roll, pitch, yaw) despite having 9 entries.

In calibration, R can also be represented as a Rodrigues rotation vector — a 3D vector whose direction is the rotation axis and whose magnitude is the rotation angle. OpenCV uses this internally.

Translation Vector t

t = (tx, ty, tz) is the position of the world origin expressed in camera coordinates. Note: t is NOT the camera position in world coordinates — that would be -RTt.

Critical distinction: The translation t is expressed in the camera's coordinate frame. The camera center in world coordinates is C = -RTt. A common mistake is confusing these two.

The Full Transformation

s is a scale factor (= Z in camera coords). After multiplication, divide u and v by the third row result.

Step-by-Step Computation

1
World → Camera: Apply [R|t]
Multiply [R|t] · [Xw, Yw, Zw, 1]T to get (Xc, Yc, Zc)
2
Normalize: Perspective divide
Compute x = Xc/Zc, y = Yc/Zc — these are normalized image coordinates on the unit plane Z=1
3
Apply distortion (if any)
Apply radial and tangential distortion to (x, y) → (xd, yd). Skip if lens is approximately ideal.
4
Camera → Pixel: Apply K
u = fx · xd + cx, v = fy · yd + cy
Homogeneous coordinates: The reason for the extra "1" appended to points and the scale factor s is that we're working in projective space. Any scalar multiple of (su, sv, s) represents the same 2D point (u, v) = (su/s, sv/s). This math elegantly unifies perspective division and linear transforms.
§07 — Lens Distortion

Lens Distortion

Real lenses are not perfect pinholes. Glass optics bend light in ways that deviate from the ideal linear model. This deviation — distortion — manifests as geometric warping of the image. The pinhole model is still used, but we add a distortion step.

🌀

Radial Distortion

The dominant effect. Rays bend radially away from (or toward) the image center. Caused by the spherical shape of lens elements.

Barrel: k₁ < 0 — image bulges outward, like a barrel. Common in wide-angle lenses.

Pincushion: k₁ > 0 — corners pinch inward. Common in telephoto lenses.

↗

Tangential Distortion

Caused by the lens not being perfectly parallel to the image sensor (tilt of lens elements). Shifts points tangentially.

Much weaker than radial distortion in well-assembled cameras. Modeled by coefficients p₁ and p₂.

Full distortion model
r² = x² + y² ← radial distance² from principal point
xd = x(1+k₁r²+k₂r⁴+k₃r⁶) + 2p₁xy + p₂(r²+2x²)
yd = y(1+k₁r²+k₂r⁴+k₃r⁶) + p₁(r²+2y²) + 2p₂xy
k₁, k₂, k₃ = radial coefficients. p₁, p₂ = tangential coefficients. OpenCV stores them as (k₁, k₂, p₁, p₂, k₃).

§08 — Calibration Procedure

The Calibration Process

To calibrate a camera, we need to observe known 3D points and their corresponding 2D pixel locations across multiple images. The most common calibration target is a checkerboard pattern.

Why a checkerboard? The inner corners (where black and white squares meet) can be detected with sub-pixel accuracy using the cornerSubPix algorithm. Their 3D positions are known exactly if we define the board coordinate system with Z=0 (flat target).

Interactive — Calibration Stepper

Calibration Workflow
Step 1: Print a calibration target

The checkerboard is placed on a flat, rigid surface. The world coordinate frame is defined with Z=0 at the board surface. Each inner corner has a known 3D position (i·sq, j·sq, 0) where sq is the square size in mm.

A typical target: 9×6 inner corners, 25mm squares. The Z=0 plane assumption makes things a homography (discussed in Zhang's method).

Step 2: Capture 15–30 images from different viewpoints

To solve for all parameters, we need the calibration target to appear in many different orientations and distances relative to the camera.

  • Cover different distances (near and far)
  • Cover all corners of the image
  • Tilt and rotate in various directions
  • Keep the target flat — do not flex it
  • Good, even lighting — no glare on the target
Step 3: Detect corners with sub-pixel accuracy

For each image, run corner detection to find the pixel positions of all inner checkerboard corners.

Python (OpenCV)
# Find coarse corners
ret, corners = cv2.findChessboardCorners(
    gray_img, (9, 6), None
)
if ret:
    # Refine to sub-pixel accuracy
    criteria = (cv2.TERM_CRITERIA_EPS + cv2.TERM_CRITERIA_MAX_ITER, 30, 0.001)
    corners_sub = cv2.cornerSubPix(
        gray_img, corners, (11,11), (-1,-1), criteria
    )

cornerSubPix uses gradient analysis to refine each corner location to sub-pixel accuracy (often to within 0.1 pixel). This is crucial for accurate calibration.

Step 4: Solve the calibration system

With N images and M corners each, we have 2NM equations (one u and one v per corner) to solve for the unknowns: K (4 params), distortion (5 params), and per-image pose (6 params × N images).

a
Compute homography H for each image
Using the Direct Linear Transform (DLT), compute a 3×3 H such that the board corners map to image corners
b
Extract K from homographies
Each homography gives 2 constraints on K. With ≥3 images we can solve the closed-form system (Zhang's method)
c
Non-linear refinement (Levenberg-Marquardt)
Minimize total reprojection error over all images simultaneously — adjusting K, dist, and all poses
Step 5: Validate with reprojection error

After calibration, re-project the known 3D corner points back onto each image using the found K, dist, and [R|t]. The reprojection error — average pixel distance between detected and re-projected corners — tells you calibration quality.

Good calibration: RMS reprojection error < 0.5 pixels for most setups. < 1.0 is generally acceptable. > 2.0 pixels usually indicates a problem (blurry images, wrong square size, insufficient coverage).
Python
# Re-project and compute error
for i in range(len(objpoints)):
    proj_pts, _ = cv2.projectPoints(
        objpoints[i], rvecs[i], tvecs[i], mtx, dist
    )
    error = cv2.norm(imgpoints[i], proj_pts, cv2.NORM_L2)
    print(f"Image {i}: {error/len(proj_pts):.3f} px RMS")
§09 — The Algorithm

Zhang's Camera Calibration Method

The standard algorithm used by OpenCV is based on Zhengyou Zhang's 1999 paper "A Flexible New Technique for Camera Calibration". It exploits the structure of calibration from planar targets (like a checkerboard at Z=0).

Step 1 — Homography from Planar Points

When the calibration target lies on the Z=0 plane, the 3D world points are of the form (X, Y, 0). Substituting Z=0 into the full projection, the third column of [R|t] drops out and we get a 2D-to-2D projective mapping — a homography H (3×3):

s
[
uv1
]
= H
[
XY1
]
, where H = K [ r₁ | r₂ | t ]
r₁, r₂ are the first two columns of R. H is computed per image using the Direct Linear Transform (DLT).

Step 2 — Solve for K (Closed Form)

Each homography H = [h₁ h₂ h₃] gives two constraints on K using the fact that r₁ and r₂ are orthonormal (columns of a rotation matrix):

h₁ᵀ B h₂ = 0← r₁ · r₂ = 0 (orthogonal)
h₁ᵀ B h₁ = h₂ᵀ B h₂← |r₁| = |r₂| = 1 (unit length)
where B = K⁻ᵀK⁻¹ is a symmetric 3×3 matrix. With n ≥ 3 images (6+ constraints), B and thus K can be recovered.

The vector b of unique elements of B satisfies a linear system Vb = 0, solved via SVD (the null space of V). Then K is extracted from B by Cholesky decomposition.

Step 3 — Recover Extrinsics and Refine

Once K is known, each per-image rotation R and translation t can be recovered from H and K. But the closed-form solution is sensitive to noise, so the final step is non-linear optimization:

Levenberg-Marquardt objective (reprojection error)
min ∑ᵢ ∑ⱼ ‖mᵢⱼ − m̂(K, dist, Rᵢ, tᵢ, Mⱼ)‖²
Minimize squared distance between detected corners mᵢⱼ and projected corners m̂ across all images i and corners j. Variables: K, dist, and all Rᵢ, tᵢ.
Why non-linear refinement? The closed-form solution ignores distortion and noise. The Levenberg-Marquardt optimizer jointly refines everything — including distortion coefficients — to minimize actual reprojection error. This is where most of the final accuracy comes from.

§10 — Implementation

OpenCV Implementation

OpenCV provides cv2.calibrateCamera() which implements Zhang's method end-to-end. Here is a complete, production-ready calibration pipeline:

Complete Calibration Script

Python · OpenCV
import cv2
import numpy as np
import glob

# ─── 1. Setup ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
BOARD_SIZE = (9, 6)    # (cols, rows) of inner corners
SQUARE_SIZE = 25.0    # physical size in mm (or any unit)

# 3D object points for one image (Z=0 plane)
objp = np.zeros((BOARD_SIZE[0] * BOARD_SIZE[1], 3), np.float32)
objp[:, :2] = np.mgrid[0:BOARD_SIZE[0], 0:BOARD_SIZE[1]].T.reshape(-1, 2)
objp *= SQUARE_SIZE

objpoints, imgpoints = [], []  # 3D and 2D point pairs
criteria = (cv2.TERM_CRITERIA_EPS + cv2.TERM_CRITERIA_MAX_ITER, 30, 0.001)

# ─── 2. Detect corners in all images ──────────────────────────────────
for fname in glob.glob('calib_images/*.jpg'):
    img = cv2.imread(fname)
    gray = cv2.cvtColor(img, cv2.COLOR_BGR2GRAY)
    ret, corners = cv2.findChessboardCorners(gray, BOARD_SIZE, None)
    if ret:
        corners2 = cv2.cornerSubPix(gray, corners, (11,11), (-1,-1), criteria)
        objpoints.append(objp)
        imgpoints.append(corners2)

# ─── 3. Calibrate ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
h, w = gray.shape
rms, K, dist, rvecs, tvecs = cv2.calibrateCamera(
    objpoints, imgpoints, (w, h), None, None
)

print(f"RMS reprojection error: {rms:.4f} pixels")
print(f"Camera matrix K:\n{K}")
print(f"Distortion coefficients: {dist.ravel()}")

# ─── 4. Undistort a new image ──────────────────────────────────────────
new_img = cv2.imread('scene.jpg')
h, w = new_img.shape[:2]

# Option A: simple undistort (may crop edges)
undistorted = cv2.undistort(new_img, K, dist)

# Option B: optimal undistort (keeps all pixels, pads with black)
new_K, roi = cv2.getOptimalNewCameraMatrix(K, dist, (w,h), 1, (w,h))
mapx, mapy = cv2.initUndistortRectifyMap(K, dist, None, new_K, (w,h), 5)
undist_opt = cv2.remap(new_img, mapx, mapy, cv2.INTER_LINEAR)

# ─── 5. Save calibration for later use ────────────────────────────────
np.savez('calibration.npz', K=K, dist=dist)
# Load: data = np.load('calibration.npz'); K = data['K']; dist = data['dist']

Common Pitfalls
!
Wrong board size
Pass inner corners count, not square count. A standard 10×7 checkerboard has 9×6 inner corners.
!
Wrong square size
The square_size unit defines the unit of your tvecs output. Use mm if you want metric results.
!
Not enough image coverage
If all images show the board at the center, cx/cy will be poorly estimated. Fill the corners of the image.
!
Blurry or glare-affected images
Sub-pixel corner accuracy is destroyed by motion blur or glare. Inspect images manually and remove bad ones.
